Case study

Affiliate journey

I led front-end research, reconnected stakeholders with their customer’s needs, and delivered product concepts resulting in a 29% jump in user engagement across 91,000 businesses.

Story

Our client – a major tech company – runs a network of 91,000 businesses. Business affiliates join to increase time-to-market, generate leads, and grow revenue. Managers act as their go-between to navigate this network. One day, they were gone. Replaced by an online self-service portal. Affiliates struggled to adapt. The client hired us to study the actors, describe new products, and recommend a path forward.

Challenge

Increase customer engagement in the self-service portal while lowering dependency on human support.

Process

Discover the current state (5w)

I cut my team’s synthesis time in half using journey map co-creation.

Research challenge

To kickoff the project, my team's first goal was to become fluent enough in our client's world to earn their partnership. We used desk research, client interviews, and a hypothesis workshop. We asked stakeholders in the workshop, “how would you describe your customer’s end-to-end experience in the affiliate network?” The session gave us proto-personas, rough journey maps, and plenty of open questions. We now wondered, "how might me use this data to explore knowledge gaps in customer interviews?"

Solution

I proposed an update to our research plan. Like the journey maps our client created, we orchestrated a co-creation exercise for participants. My team agreed that this method would allow us to test several hypotheses.

Outcome

  1. ➊ How affiliates negotiated their journey was less about personas and more about context. Behaviors and values shifted based on circumstance. We coined these phenomena Mindsets.
  2. ➋ Affiliates took nonlinear paths in their journey maps. Sometimes they got stuck on the back roads, other times they cruised on the freeway. This forced them to travel in roundabout ways and take on new mindsets. But why? The answers came together on our affinity board.
  3. ➌ Affiliates were effected by conditions out of their control, but caused by our client unintentionally. Managers had higher priorities so they took a back seat. Sales tools had bugs that never got fixed causing leads to vanish. Weeks went by without support. Uncertainty got in the way of aspirations.

Describe the future state (2.5 weeks)

I fought to share the customer’s perspective with a board game.

Workshop challenge

Here's the messy the part (you gotta have a messy part, right?). We tried to shoehorn our interview synthesis board into a journey map-esque spreadsheet. Helpful for identifying milestones. Not so helpful if the route customers take to get to the milestones looks like a roller coaster. What we learned about Affiliates required pause for an alternative deliverable. Not a journey map, like the one engraved in the statement of work. No. Something like a board game that forced players to experience the frustrations voiced by Affiliates.

Our internal leadership balked at the pitch. But often the spirit of research is to welcome the occasional raised eyebrow. It means you're on to something. With constructive dialog we found a compromise together. By walking the client through our findings, they would need to arrive at a similar conclusion. The client’s exact words were, “this could be a game.” It was t-minus one week to prepare a finished product.

Solution

I’ve struggled in the past with behemoth synthesis sessions. I championed just-in-time synthesis. The rolling debriefs sped up the bid to sprinkle customer quotes across the milestones on game board. We held a working prototype session in the studio painting dice. Through open invites to play the game, we laughed when players hit landmines (losing a turn), and riffed until it was ready to go to print.

Outcome

  1. ➊ Tactile play made it easier to consume an enormous volume of information in a brief span of time.
  2. ➋ Clients sought out copies of the game to "take home" and play with their respective departments. (We camouflaged the board game, matching the style with the client's brand guidelines in an effort to infiltrate hearts and minds.)
  3. ➌ Fjord was contacted later to host the game at a leadership retreat

Reflection

The client warned, "our organization is suspicious of outside research.” We chose to use desk research and in-depth interviews to provide several data points that grounded the evidence in convergent validity. Given more time, I would have liked to walk peripheral clients through our analyses.

After 11 years at this company, this board game was the most informative and fun activity I’ve been through. — Client Participant

Define the path forward (2.5 weeks)

I guided new team members through the delivery of 7 concepts.

Final deliverable challenge

Packing 5 weeks of research into actionable recommendations sounds daunting. Thankfully, we followed a collaborative process with our client. Following the game activity, the workshop teams stepped through a series of exercises. They iterated mockups, then prioritized twelve winners. Over two weeks, my team worked with the client to bring those twelve ideas into the polished form you see here. My personal challenge was taking on a transitional leadership role. While two new colleagues came aboard, three more left the team.

Trever was the glue holding the two phases of the project together. He was my personal go-to for input I needed to move forward. He owned the final deliverables, providing content, useful visual direction, and stellar copy.— Ki, Senior Visual Designer

Solution

Is a designer a copywriter? The concept storyboards needed to speak to business people, product owners, developers and other designers. With that audience in mind, I worked with our Design Director and Senior Visual Designer. We turned sketched concepts into polished storyboards. Our concepts spoke to the impact on both the business and the customer, plotted against a two-year roadmap.

Outcome

Several concepts have gone into production. First quarter reports showed a 29% increase in Affiliate engagement. Our recommendations laid the groundwork. The client is adopting new modes of listening and learning from their customer. We instilled the confidence required to make more effective future improvements to their Affiliate network.

Next steps

One month later, we were back at it, presenting the board game to our client’s leadership team. Requests to “play the game” continued to roll in.
