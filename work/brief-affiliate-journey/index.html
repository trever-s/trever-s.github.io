Case study

Affiliate journey

I led front-end research, reconnected stakeholders with their customer’s needs, and delivered product concepts resulting in a 29% jump in user engagement across 91,000 businesses.

Story

Imagine a SaaS product that lets you develop your business on the backbone of a name brand technology. One day, your assigned manager can no longer support your growth as an Affiliate in this network. The manager is replaced by a set of instructions to a self-service portal with a suite of marketing and sales tools. Affiliates were not thrilled. The survey data that backed this initiative also wasn’t robust enough to explain why it was failing. Fjord was hired to round out this quantitative puzzle using qualitative research to recommend a path forward.

Challenge

Increase autonomous engagement in the network while lowering Affiliates' dependency on human support.

Background

My Role

Design Research Lead; interim Design Lead. Team included one design director, two interaction designers, one project manager, one visual designer, one solutions architect, and one account lead.

Responsibilities

Operations, desk research, expert, stakeholder, customer interviews (in-depth pair, group, and single person), synthesis, artifacts, mindsets, workshop design and facilitation, product storyboards, concepting, the final deliverable.

Research

1500 surveys reviewed, thirty-four interviews conducted, two workshops hosted, competitive analysis, literature reviews, landscape scans.

Process

Discover the current state (5w)

I cut my team’s synthesis time in half using journey map co-creation.

Research challenge

We asked the client in the first week, “how would you describe your customer’s experience as an affiliate in the network?” To find out, my team ran a hypothesis workshop. The session gave us proto-personas, sticky note journey maps, and plenty of open questions client's had about their customers. Now we wondered, how might me use this data to explore knowledge gaps in our upcoming interviews?

Solution

I altered our customer research plan when my team agreed to include a journey map co-creation exercise similar to what we'd done in the hypothesis workshop. This was the perfect opportunity to test what we'd learned from our client. Logistically, rather than rely on our team to piece together each customer’s journey from transcripts, we could also produce the deliverable during the session.

Outcome

  1. ➊ We learned that older customers started their businesses using the Affiliate network to gain a strategic edge — newer customers felt more network-agnostic.
  2. ➋ The format encouraged participants to recount past stories, yielding a follow-up question framework, and produced artifacts that sped up post-interview debrief times from half-hour to fifteen minutes
  3. ➌ The interviews supported hypotheses about customer's motivations that were later substantiated when we analyzed the maps in aggregate

Next steps

Our team's cumulative understanding of customer behavior was about to unleash our insight: circuitous routes. "Circuitous what?" Affiliates, no surprise, shirked convention. They did the opposite of traveling in a linear pathway, moving along the journey backward, forward, even roundabout loops. This jolted the client’s understanding of their Affiliates, paving the way for an unusual deliverable.

Reflection

Research performed in complex time envelopes is about making the right trade-offs. A constraint we dealt with was a lack of candidates who were newer customers. In hindsight, we'd have sought to understand how time affects the collected data with a longitudinal diary study.

Trever has the unique ability to get people to share their authentic feelings with him — a skill that was beneficial during our interviews. He exudes a calm presence that instills confidence in his interviewees and teammates.— Christine, Interaction Designer

Describe the future state (2.5 weeks)

I fought to share the customer’s perspective with a board game.

Workshop challenge

A valiant attempt to record the affiliate's journey in a spreadsheet raised eyebrows. How might we assert that Affiliate mindsets shifted based on circumstance? My colleague and I proposed a journey map with game mechanics that simulated this effect on a board game. Leadership balked. But often the spirit of research is to challenge sacred cows. With constructive dialog, we found a compromise: if we walk the client through our findings, and they arrive at a similar conclusion, we had their blessing. The client’s exact words were, “this could be a game.” We had one week to prepare a finished product.

Solution

I’ve struggled in the past with behemoth synthesis sessions. This time I championed just-in-time synthesis, a rolling debrief which sped up the bid to compile customer quotes on the board game. More push back again meant we redoubled our effort, holding a working prototype session in the studio. Through open invitations to play the game, we laughed and riffed until it was ready to go to print.

Outcome

  1. ➊ Tactile play made it easier to consume an enormous volume of information in a brief span of time
  2. ➋ Clients sought copies of the game to play later with their respective departments
  3. ➌ Fjord was contacted later to host the game at a leadership retreat

Next steps

The workshop teams were ready to seize this newfound empathy from the game. We moved through a series of exercises. Iterated mockups. Prioritized twelve winners. Over two weeks, my team worked with the client to bring those ideas into their finished form. Our efforts resulted in a final deliverable, a toothsome slide deck full of concept storyboards.

Reflection

The client felt suspicious. Their “organization held misgivings about outside research.” It was awesome that we had developed a rapport and they felt willing to share this detail with our team. Given a time machine, I would have walked workshop participants through our analyses, which grounded our evidence in convergent validity using several data points.

After 11 years, this was the most informative and fun activity I’ve been through.— Client Participant

Define the path forward (2.5 weeks)

I guided new team members through the delivery of 7 concepts.

Final deliverable challenge

Even in the fuzzy front-end, I try to communicate our process to clients. Sure, it’s occasionally a dog and pony show. It's overshadowed by my genuine enthusiasm for learning about people. Stepping into a transitional leadership role, I took the same ethos to heart. While two new team members came aboard in the final three weeks, three other members left. I stepped in to become the teams interim design lead. It was easy. I worked with amazing professionals.

Solution

Is a designer a copywriter? The concept storyboards weren’t going to write themselves. We turned sketched concepts into polished storyboards. Along with concepts, we told the story of how we reached our recommendations and how they could be developed on an incremental roadmap.

This information lived in a hand-off document. A design exercise in and of itself. We had to consider how to ensure its effectiveness. The audience for the document is an unknown. It will show the reader how you considered their business from the inside out, examined their organizational capabilities, and became intimate with their customer’s unmet needs.

Outcome

Several concepts have gone into production. First quarter reports showed a 29% increase in Affiliate engagement. Our recommendations laid the groundwork. The client is adopting new modes of listening and learning from their customer. We instilled the confidence required to make more effective future improvements to their Affiliate network.

Next steps

One month later, we were back at it, presenting the board game to our client’s leadership team. Requests to “play the game” continued to roll in.
