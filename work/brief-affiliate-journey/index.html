Case study

Affiliate journey

In a dark hour for a major tech company and their loyal customer base, I led front-end research, reconnected stakeholders with their customer's needs, and delivered product concepts resulting in a 29% jump in user engagement across 91,000 businesses.

• •

Story

Our client is a household name, who alongside other pursuits hosts a business-to-business subscription network. Organizations partner up with our client to take advantage of their robust technology offerings. Two years prior, human support was removed across the network for the majority of non-enterprise affiliates and replaced with a self-service online portal. The portal – conceived mostly by survey data feedback – was failing to help customer's accomplish their goals. Fjord was hired over 10 weeks to conduct robust generative user research, closely collaborate with the client's stakeholders, and deliver concepts on a realistic release timeline.

Challenge

Increase autonomous engagement for high-touch businesses and lower the dependency on human support.

Background

My Role

Design Researcher, Lead Interviewer, and Interim Design Lead. Team was comprised of two interaction designers, one project manager, one visual designer, one solutions architect, and one account lead.

Responsibilities

Operations, desk research, expert interviews, stakeholder interviews, in-depth pair, group, and single person interviews, synthesis, artifacts, mindsets, workshop design and facilitation, concepting, product storyboards, and the final presentation.

Research

1500 partners surveyed, thirty-four interviews, three workshops, competitive analysis, literature reviews, and landscape scans.

A word on processA mentor of mine begins workshops by outlining the design process for IDEO, the Standford d.school, and Frog. The reveal? They're all similar when you strip away the veneer.

No question, the design process lends non-designers a model for how our work delivers value. But as designers, it's our job to scrutinize our approach. We must regularly ask "are we adapting based on our observations or just stepping through the motions?"

I've focused on three snapshots during this project which demonstrate my ability to stay flexible and provide value where it would have been missed otherwise.

Process

Discover the current state (5w)

I pioneered a remote interview activity cutting my team’s synthesis time in half.

Research challenge

One of the challenges we addressed head on was disambiguating the user's current state journey. We hosted a ”hypothesis workshop” with stakeholders to faciliate the co-creation of customer journey maps. But we noticed particpants struggled to piece together a cohesive narrative for their customers.

Solution

To solve the mystery, I modified our research plan and designed an activity to engage particpants in storytelling and visualization. As the lead interviewer, I walked my team through the proposal, gathered feedback, and implemented the framework with an online whiteboarding tool.

Outcome

This modification fulfilled three objectives:

  1. ➊ Provide users with a visualization to tell their story (use this as a basis for follow-up questions)
  2. ➋ Materialize real-time documentation the team can use in post-interview debriefs and synthesis
  3. ➌ Identify hypothetical user types based on needs, motivations, and pain-points

Next steps

The co-creation journey exercise surfaced a crucial insight: customers don't travel in a linear path seen on conventional journey maps. Instead, they move forward, backward, and even in serpentine loops to navigate the ecosystem. This discovery jolted the client's understanding of their customer, paving the way for a surprising deliverable.

Reflection

Research performed in complex time envelopes is often about making the right trade-offs. A constraint we dealt with was a lack of canidates who were newer customers. In hindsight, we may have proposed a longitudinial diary study to understand how time affects the data being collected.

Describe the future state (2.5w)

I advocated for a deliverable that became the turning point for the project's success.

Workshop challenge

When we first mapped out the affiliate journey we realized it had to be from their perspective. It needed to take into account 1500 surveys, 12 in-depth interviews, 12 co-created journey maps, and 12 in-depths stakeholder interviews.

To reflect their unique experiences we proposed a journey map turned interactive board game. Leadership balked. But often the spirit of research is to challenge sacred cows. With constructive dialogue, we found a compromise: walk the client through our findings, if they arrive at the same conclusions, we had the green light from leadership. The client's exact words were, ”this could be a game.“ We had one week to develop a finished product for our mid-way workshop.

Solution

I've struggled in the past with behemoth synthesis sessions. This time I championed just-in-time synthesis, a mini and daily rolling debrief which accelerated our ability to compile quotes for the board game. Still receving some pushback internally, we redoubled our effort holding a working prototype session ahead of schedule. Through iterative gameplay with unsuspecting studio mates, we laughed, tweaked, and riffed until the game was ready for the printing press.

Outcome

The board game was not just a journey map with a pair of dice. It packed an emotional punch – a microcosmic whiff of real life encounters with customers.

The game achieved three objectives:

  1. ➊ Tactile play made it easier to take in a large volume of information in a short span of time
  2. ➋ People sought out copies of the game to take back to their respective departments
  3. ➌ Fjord was hired again to share our research and host the game at a leadership retreat

Next steps

The remainder of the day saw a convergence of minds co-creating and prioritizing twelve key concepts. We took these concepts further, working closely with the client to itterate on their final form over the next two weeks. This resulted in a fifty-page deck with seven concept storyboards, detailing minimum and long-term integration strategies on a product roadmap.

Reflection

The client had a long history of using quantitative data to inform and guide their decision-making processess. Clearly though, something was hindering their ability to translate survey feedback into successful products. Our desk research mixed with qualitative inquiry gave us at least two data points to show the evidence we gathered was grounded in convergent validity. Spending more time emphasizing this approach may have altered the client's long-term design research strategy.

After 11 years here, I can honestly say this was, by far, the most informative and fun activity I’ve been through!— Client Participant

Define the path forward (2.5w)

I guided team members through the production of the final deliverable.

Final deliverable challenge

One of the things I learned at Fjord is even in the generative phase, we’re less passive and more proactive about sensemaking, recommendations, and collaboration. When two new team members came on board in the final three weeks of the program, while concurrently, three other members left the team, I stepped in and became the team's overall lead.

Solution

I demonstrated transitional leadership, ensuring the new Design Director and Senior Visual Designer were looped into conversations (we had remote team members) and that they were clear on our final deliverables. Working over several weeks internally and with our client, we refined the concepts from our workshops, turning them into detailed storyboards through the lens of the business, the user (mindsets), and the product owner and developer.

Outcome

The work has since gone into production with many of our concepts responsible for an engagement increase of 29% in the first quarter. We laid the groundwork that allowed our client to adopt new modes of listening and learning from their customer. Giving them the confidence required to make less risky future product and service improvements.

Next steps

One month after the project ended we were back at it again presenting the board game to our client’s leadership team. Seventy people attended the event and it was described by many as a 'casino floor' like atmosophere. The event made waves and before I left Fjord, client requests to "play the game" continued to roll in.

Reflection

For consultants, the proposal and the statement of work chart the project map – and write the check – before everything is underway. And as it so often does, our exploratory research unearthed nascent insights that demanded a new heading. I'm proud of this work because both parties displayed a willingness to pursue uncomfortable truths for the sake of the customer's well-being. Research that travels far must come from a place of collective ownership within an organization.
